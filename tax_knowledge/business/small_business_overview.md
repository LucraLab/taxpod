# Small Business Entity Types and Tax Treatment

**Primary IRS References:** Publication 535, Publication 3402, Form 2553, Schedule C, Form 1065, Form 1120-S

---

## Overview

Choosing the right entity type is one of the most consequential tax decisions for a small business. The structure determines how income is taxed, whether self-employment (SE) tax applies, and what deductions are available.

---

## Sole Proprietorship

### Tax Treatment
- Income and expenses reported on **Schedule C (Form 1040)**
- Net profit flows to Form 1040 and is subject to:
  - **Ordinary income tax** at your marginal rate
  - **Self-employment (SE) tax** at 15.3% on net profit

### SE Tax on Sole Proprietors
- SE tax rate: **15.3%** on net self-employment income up to the Social Security wage base ($176,100 for 2025)
- Above wage base: **2.9%** Medicare only (plus 0.9% Additional Medicare Tax if income > $200k single / $250k MFJ)
- Deduction: deduct **half of SE tax** as an above-the-line deduction (Form 1040, Schedule 1)

### Characteristics
- Simplest structure — no separate return required
- **No liability protection** — personal assets at risk
- All business income subject to SE tax (no salary/distribution split)
- Best for: freelancers, consultants, side businesses with modest income

---

## Single-Member LLC (SMLLC)

### Tax Treatment
- **Disregarded entity** — treated identically to a sole proprietorship for federal tax purposes
- Still reports on Schedule C
- Still subject to full SE tax on net profit
- **Provides liability protection** (state law) without changing federal tax treatment
- Can elect to be treated as a corporation (Form 8832) or S-corp (Form 2553)

---

## Multi-Member LLC (Partnership)

### Tax Treatment
- Default classification: **partnership**
- Files **Form 1065** (informational return)
- Issues **Schedule K-1** to each member showing their share of income/loss
- Each member reports K-1 income on their personal return
- Members who actively participate are subject to SE tax on their distributive share

### Characteristics
- Flexibility in profit/loss allocation (unlike S-corp's strict pro-rata requirement)
- Can elect S-corp or C-corp treatment by filing Form 8832 (+ Form 2553 for S-corp)
- Partners are generally subject to SE tax on guaranteed payments and their share of business income

## S-Corporation

### Tax Treatment
The S-corporation is typically the **most tax-advantaged structure** for profitable small businesses due to SE tax savings.

- **Pass-through taxation** — the S-corp itself pays no federal income tax
- Shareholders report their pro-rata share of S-corp income on **Schedule K-1**
- Income flows to Form 1040 and is taxed at the shareholder's ordinary rate
- **Distributions to shareholders are NOT subject to SE tax or FICA**
- Only **W-2 wages** paid to owner-employees are subject to payroll taxes (FICA)

### Reasonable Compensation Requirement
The IRS requires S-corp owner-employees to pay themselves a **"reasonable" salary** before taking distributions.

- Failure to pay reasonable compensation is the #1 audit trigger for S-corps
- IRS standard: what would you pay a third party to perform your services?
- Cannot pay $0 salary and take all income as distributions
- Reasonable salary factors: industry norms, duties performed, hours worked, comparable wages in the area

**SE Tax Savings Example:**
- S-corp net profit: $200,000
- Reasonable salary set at: $100,000
- Salary subject to FICA: $100,000 × 15.3% = $15,300 (split between employer and employee)
- Remaining $100,000 as distribution: $0 FICA
- **Annual savings vs. sole proprietorship:** ~$15,300

### S-Corp Election
- File **Form 2553** (Election by a Small Business Corporation)
- **Deadline:** March 15 of the tax year the election is to take effect, OR within 75 days of formation
- Late elections may be granted relief under Rev. Proc. 2013-30 (with valid business reasons)
- LLC can elect S-corp by filing both Form 8832 and Form 2553

### S-Corp Requirements
- Domestic corporation only
- Maximum **100 shareholders**
- Only **one class of stock** (differences in voting rights are permitted)
- Shareholders must be U.S. citizens or resident aliens (no nonresident aliens)
- No corporate shareholders or partnership shareholders (most LLCs are fine)

### S-Corp Compliance Costs
- Separate tax return: **Form 1120-S** (due March 15)
- Payroll processing required: **Forms 941** (quarterly), **940** (annual FUTA)
- State registration and annual reports
- Separate bank account and corporate formalities

### When S-Corp Makes Sense
- Generally worthwhile when net business profit exceeds **$80,000–$100,000/year**
- Below this threshold, payroll compliance costs may exceed SE tax savings

## C-Corporation

### Tax Treatment
- Pays corporate income tax at **flat 21% rate** (post-TCJA)
- **Double taxation:** profits taxed at corporate level; dividends taxed again at shareholder level (0%, 15%, or 20% qualified dividend rate)
- Files **Form 1120** (due April 15, or March 15 with fiscal year)

### When C-Corp Makes Sense
- Seeking venture capital (VC typically requires C-corp structure)
- Retaining profits in the company at 21% rate (vs. higher personal rates)
- Issuing stock options (ISOs only available in C-corp)
- Planning to go public

### Generally NOT recommended for:
- Small service businesses
- Pass-through income situations
- Businesses planning to distribute most profits to owners

## Entity Comparison Summary

| Feature | Sole Prop / SMLLC | S-Corp | C-Corp |
|---|---|---|---|
| Federal return | Schedule C | Form 1120-S | Form 1120 |
| SE/payroll tax | All profit | W-2 wages only | W-2 wages |
| Pass-through | Yes | Yes | No |
| QBI deduction | Yes | Yes | No |
| Liability protection | No (SMLLC: Yes) | Yes | Yes |
| Compliance complexity | Low | Medium | High |
| VC-compatible | No | No | Yes |
