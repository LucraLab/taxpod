# Deductible Business Expenses — Section 162

**Primary IRS Reference:** Section 162, Publication 535, Schedule C / Form 1120-S

---

## The Fundamental Rule

Under **Section 162**, a business may deduct all expenses that are:

1. **Ordinary** — common and accepted in your industry
2. **Necessary** — helpful and appropriate for the business (not required to be indispensable)
3. **Paid or incurred** during the tax year in carrying on a trade or business

Both conditions must be met. Personal expenses are never deductible, and expenses with a dual personal/business purpose must be allocated.

## Common Deductible Business Expenses

### Advertising and Marketing
- Online ads (Google, Meta, LinkedIn)
- Website design and hosting
- SEO services and content marketing
- Brand design, logos, copywriting
- Promotional materials, business cards
- Sponsorships with clear business purpose
- Social media management

### Bank Fees
- Monthly account maintenance fees
- Wire transfer fees
- Merchant processing fees (Stripe, Square)
- NSF fees on business accounts
- Loan origination fees (amortized over loan life)

### Business Insurance
- General liability insurance
- Professional liability / E&O insurance
- Cyber liability insurance
- Key-person life insurance (if business is beneficiary)
- Workers' compensation insurance
- Directors and officers (D&O) insurance

### Business Meals
- **50% deductible** — meals with clients, prospects, or partners where business is discussed
- Must document: who attended, business purpose, date, location, amount
- **0% deductible** — entertainment expenses (concerts, sporting events) — eliminated by TCJA (2018)
- **100% deductible** — meals provided on business premises for employer's convenience; company-wide holiday parties

### Contract Labor / Independent Contractors
- Payments to 1099 contractors, freelancers, agencies
- Fully deductible in year paid
- **Form 1099-NEC required** if paying an individual or unincorporated entity $600 or more in a calendar year (due January 31)
- Failure to file 1099s does NOT disallow the deduction, but may result in penalties

### Home Office Deduction
Available if you use a portion of your home **regularly and exclusively** for business.

**Simplified Method:**
- $5 per square foot, maximum 300 sq ft
- Maximum deduction: **$1,500/year**
- No depreciation recapture risk

**Actual Expense Method:**
- Calculate business-use percentage: office sq ft ÷ total home sq ft
- Apply percentage to: rent or mortgage interest, utilities, repairs, homeowner's insurance, depreciation
- May produce a larger deduction than simplified method
- **Depreciation recapture** applies when home is sold

> **Exclusive use rule:** The space must be used ONLY for business — a home office that doubles as a guest room does NOT qualify.
>
> **S-corp owners:** You cannot deduct home office directly on the S-corp return unless the corporation reimburses you under an Accountable Plan.

### Internet and Phone
- Business portion of internet and cell phone bills
- If exclusively business: 100% deductible
- If mixed personal/business: deduct only the business-use percentage
- Separate business phone line: 100% deductible

### Legal and Professional Fees
- Attorney fees (contracts, IP, employment, business formation)
- CPA and tax preparation fees (business portion)
- Business consulting fees
- HR and compliance consulting
- **Non-deductible:** Legal fees for acquiring a business or capital asset (added to basis)

### Office Supplies
- Paper, pens, printer ink, toner
- Staples, envelopes, labels
- Small tools and equipment under the $2,500 safe harbor
- Postage and shipping

### Professional Development and Subscriptions
- Industry conferences and seminars
- Online courses and certifications
- Trade association memberships
- Business-related books and publications
- SaaS subscriptions used for business operations (see Software below)

### Rent
- Office space, co-working memberships
- Equipment rentals
- Storage units for business property
- Fully deductible in year paid (cash-basis businesses)

### Salaries and Wages
- Employee salaries and wages (W-2)
- Payroll taxes (employer's share of FICA — 7.65%)
- Employee benefits (health insurance, retirement matching)
- **S-corp owner salary:** Reasonable W-2 compensation is deductible by the S-corp (reduces corporate-level income)
- Bonuses and commissions to employees

### Software and SaaS Subscriptions
- Fully deductible under **Section 162** or **Section 179**
- Examples: Slack, Notion, GitHub, AWS, Google Workspace, OpenAI API, HubSpot, QuickBooks
- Off-the-shelf software purchased outright: may elect Section 179 immediate expensing
- **No capitalization required** for subscription-based software (operating expense)

### Travel (Business Purpose)
- Airfare, train, or other transportation
- Hotel accommodations
- 50% of meals while traveling
- Taxi, Uber, rental car at destination
- Must have a **primary business purpose** (personal days tacked on are not deductible)
- **Commuting is NOT deductible** — travel from home to regular place of business

### Vehicle Expenses
Two methods — choose the one that produces the greater deduction (and stick with it for that vehicle):

**Standard Mileage Rate:**
- 2024: **$0.67 per mile** for business miles
- 2025: IRS will announce updated rate (check Rev. Proc. or Notice)
- Must maintain a mileage log (date, destination, business purpose, miles)

**Actual Expense Method:**
- Deduct business percentage of: gas, insurance, repairs, registration, depreciation
- Business-use percentage = business miles ÷ total miles driven
- May be combined with bonus depreciation or Section 179 on the vehicle itself (see `section_179_bonus_depreciation.md`)

## Startup Costs — Section 195

Business startup costs incurred before the business opens its doors are governed by **Section 195**.

### Qualifying Startup Costs
- Market research and feasibility studies
- Legal fees for business formation
- Advertising before opening
- Training employees before opening
- Travel to investigate potential business locations

### Deduction Rules

| Total Startup Costs | Year-One Deduction | Remainder |
|---|---|---|
| Under $50,000 | Up to $5,000 in year business begins | Amortize over 180 months (15 years) |
| $50,001–$55,000 | Phase-out: $5,000 reduced dollar-for-dollar | Amortize remainder over 180 months |
| Over $55,000 | $0 first-year deduction | Amortize entire amount over 180 months |

### Organizational Costs (Section 248 for Corps / Section 709 for Partnerships)
- Legal fees to form the corporation or partnership
- State filing fees
- Same $5,000 / $50,000 rules apply (parallel treatment to Section 195)

**Example:**
- LucraLab incurred $20,000 in startup costs before launching
- Year 1 deduction: $5,000
- Remaining $15,000 ÷ 180 months = $83.33/month amortized over 15 years

## Non-Deductible Business Expenses

| Expense | Reason Not Deductible |
|---|---|
| Capital expenditures (equipment, property) | Must depreciate — see Section 179 / 168(k) |
| Personal expenses | Not ordinary/necessary for business |
| Commuting costs | Specifically excluded by IRS |
| Fines and penalties (to government) | Public policy disallowance |
| Political contributions | Not ordinary/necessary |
| Club memberships (social, athletic) | Specifically excluded by Section 274 |
| Client entertainment | Eliminated by TCJA (0% deductible) |
| Federal income taxes paid | Not deductible |

---

## Documentation Requirements

The IRS requires contemporaneous records for all business expenses:
- **Receipts** for expenses over $75 (recommended for all)
- **Credit card and bank statements** (supplemental)
- **Mileage log** for vehicle deductions
- **Meal receipts** with notation of who attended and business purpose
- **Home office measurements** if claiming actual expense method
