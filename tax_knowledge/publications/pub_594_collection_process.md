# IRS Publication 594: The IRS Collection Process

> **Reference:** IRS Pub 594 — The IRS Collection Process  
> **Scope:** Federal tax debt collection timeline, notices, liens, levies, and collection alternatives  
> **Last Reviewed:** 2025

---

## Overview

When a taxpayer owes federal taxes and does not pay in full by the due date, the IRS initiates the collection process. Understanding this process is critical for advising clients on timing, rights, and the best resolution strategy.

---

## Collection Timeline: Notice → Lien → Levy

```
Tax Due → CP14 (First Notice) → CP501 → CP503 → CP504 → LT11 (Final Notice)
                                                              ↓ (30 days)
                                              CDP Hearing or Levy Begins
```

### Step 1: Initial Balance Due Notice
- **CP14** — "Balance Due" notice. First formal notice of tax owed.
- This is the **assessment date trigger** — the 10-year CSED clock starts here.
- Taxpayer has ~30 days to respond before follow-up notices begin.

### Step 2: Follow-Up Notices
- **CP501** — First reminder. Balance still owed.
- **CP503** — Second reminder. Urgency increases.
- **CP504** — "Intent to Levy" on state tax refunds. IRS can levy state refunds at this stage.

### Step 3: Final Notice Before Levy
- **LT11 (or Letter 1058)** — "Final Notice of Intent to Levy and Notice of Your Right to a Hearing"
- **This is the critical notice.** Taxpayer has **30 days** to respond before levy action begins.
- Triggers CDP (Collection Due Process) hearing rights.

## Federal Tax Lien

A **Notice of Federal Tax Lien (NFTL)** is a public legal claim against a taxpayer's property.

| Fact | Detail |
|------|--------|
| When filed | Automatically after 10 days of unpaid CP14 (if balance > $10,000) |
| What it encumbers | All property and rights to property (real estate, vehicles, financial accounts) |
| Public record | Yes — filed with county recorder, searchable by creditors |
| Credit impact | Significant — appears on credit reports |
| When released | Within 30 days of full payment or satisfaction |
| Subordination | IRS can subordinate lien to allow refinancing (Form 14134) |
| Withdrawal | IRS can withdraw lien under certain conditions (Form 12277) |

**Key point:** A lien is a legal claim; a levy is the actual seizure of assets. Lien ≠ Levy.

---

## Tax Levy

A **tax levy** is the IRS's legal seizure of property to satisfy a tax debt.

### What the IRS Can Seize
- **Wages** — IRS sends Form 668-W to employer; employer must withhold
- **Bank accounts** — One-time seizure of funds on date of levy (21-day hold period for bank levies)
- **Accounts receivable** — For business owners
- **Social Security benefits** — Up to 15% via Federal Payment Levy Program (FPLP)
- **Real property** — Homes, land (requires additional approval)
- **Retirement accounts** — Yes, including IRAs and 401(k)s
- **Vehicles, equipment, other personal property**

### Levy Exemptions (Property IRS Cannot Levy)
- Unemployment benefits
- Workers' compensation
- Certain disability payments
- Minimum wage exemption for wages (IRS tables define exempt amount)
- Personal effects and household goods up to $10,370 (2024, inflation-adjusted)

### Bank Levy Process
1. IRS sends levy notice to bank
2. Bank freezes funds for **21 days** (allows taxpayer to resolve)
3. If unresolved after 21 days, funds remitted to IRS

## Collection Alternatives Summary

When a taxpayer cannot pay in full, several alternatives exist:

| Alternative | Best For | Key Requirement |
|-------------|----------|-----------------|
| **Installment Agreement (IA)** | Can pay over time | Ability to make monthly payments |
| **Offer in Compromise (OIC)** | Debt exceeds ability to pay | RCP < total liability |
| **Currently Not Collectible (CNC)** | Temporary hardship | Income < allowable expenses |
| **Penalty Abatement** | Reduce total balance | 3 years clean compliance (FTA) |
| **Innocent Spouse Relief** | Joint liability dispute | Didn't know about understatement |

> **See:** `tax_knowledge/resolution/collection_alternatives.md` for full detail on each option.

## Taxpayer Rights in the Collection Process

### 30-Day Window After LT11
- Upon receiving LT11 (Final Notice), taxpayer has **30 days** to:
  1. Pay in full
  2. Enter an installment agreement
  3. Submit an Offer in Compromise
  4. Request a CDP hearing
- Missing this window does NOT eliminate rights, but levy action can begin

### Collection Due Process (CDP) Hearing Rights
- **Triggered by:** LT11 or Notice of Federal Tax Lien filing
- **File Form 12153** — Request for a Collection Due Process or Equivalent Hearing
- **Deadline:** 30 days from the date on the notice
- **Effect:** Suspends levy action while hearing is pending
- **CSED tolling:** CDP hearing tolls the CSED clock during the hearing period
- **What you can raise at CDP:**
  - Challenges to the appropriateness of the collection action
  - Collection alternatives (IA, OIC, CNC)
  - Spousal defenses
  - Challenges to the underlying liability (if no prior opportunity to dispute)
- **Appeals:** After CDP hearing, can appeal to U.S. Tax Court within 30 days

### Equivalent Hearing
- If the 30-day CDP deadline is missed (but within 1 year of notice), taxpayer can request an **Equivalent Hearing**
- Does NOT suspend levy; does NOT preserve Tax Court rights
- Still useful for negotiating collection alternatives

## Quick Reference: Timing Summary

| Event | Timing |
|-------|--------|
| CSED begins | Assessment date (CP14 issuance / return due date) |
| NFTL filed | 10+ days after unpaid CP14 |
| CP504 (state levy) | After multiple prior notices |
| LT11 issued | Before federal levy — final warning |
| CDP deadline | 30 days from LT11 |
| Bank levy hold | 21 days before funds remitted |
| Lien release | Within 30 days of full payment |
