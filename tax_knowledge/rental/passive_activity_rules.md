# Passive Activity Loss (PAL) Rules — Rental Property

**Primary IRS Reference:** Section 469, Publication 527, Form 8582

---

## Overview

Under the **passive activity loss rules (Section 469)**, rental activities are classified as **passive by default**, regardless of how much time the landlord spends managing them.

**The fundamental rule:** Passive losses can only offset **passive income**. They generally cannot offset wages, salaries, interest, dividends, or other "active" income.

---

## Rental Activities Are Passive — Default Rule

Even if you spend significant time managing your rental properties, rental income and losses are passive unless you qualify for one of the exceptions below.

**Passive income includes:**
- Net rental income from other rental properties
- Income from limited partnerships
- Income from other passive business activities

**Passive losses cannot offset:**
- W-2 wages and salaries
- Self-employment income
- Interest and dividends
- Capital gains (generally)
- Business income from activities you materially participate in

---

## Exception 1 — The $25,000 Active Participation Allowance

### The Rule

Taxpayers who **actively participate** in a rental activity can deduct up to **$25,000** of rental losses against ordinary income each year.

### Active Participation (Lower Bar)

"Active participation" is a **lower standard** than "material participation." You qualify if you:

- Make management decisions in a bona fide sense
- Approve tenants and rental terms
- Approve major repairs or capital expenditures
- Set rental rates

You do NOT need to do the day-to-day work. Hiring a property manager is fine as long as you make the key decisions. You must own at least a **10% interest** in the rental activity.

### Phase-Out Based on AGI

The $25,000 allowance phases out as Modified Adjusted Gross Income (MAGI) increases:

| MAGI | Available Allowance |
|---|---|
| $100,000 or less | Full $25,000 |
| $100,001 – $149,999 | $25,000 reduced by $0.50 for each $1 over $100,000 |
| $150,000 or more | **$0 — completely phased out** |

**Phase-out formula:** Allowance = $25,000 − [($MAGI − $100,000) × 0.5]

**Example:**
- AGI: $120,000
- Reduction: ($120,000 − $100,000) × 0.5 = $10,000
- Available allowance: $25,000 − $10,000 = **$15,000**

### Married Filing Separately

- If you lived with your spouse at any time during the year: **$0 allowance** (complete disallowance)
- If you lived apart from your spouse for the entire year: up to $12,500

## Exception 2 — Real Estate Professional Status

### The Rule

If you qualify as a **real estate professional**, your rental activities are treated as **non-passive**, meaning rental losses can offset any type of income without limitation.

### Qualification Requirements

You must meet **BOTH** of the following tests (Section 469(c)(7)):

1. **750-hour test:** More than 750 hours per year in real property trades or businesses in which you materially participate
2. **Primary occupation test:** Time in real property trades or businesses exceeds time in all other trades or businesses combined

**Real property trades or businesses** include:
- Real estate development, construction, acquisition
- Rental management and operation
- Property sales (real estate agent/broker)
- Real estate financing

### Additional Requirements

- Must **materially participate** in each rental activity (or make a grouping election)
- **Material participation** for each property: generally 500+ hours/year, or one of 7 IRS tests
- Simply owning rental properties managed by others is NOT sufficient

### Practical Reality

Real estate professional status is **very rare** for W-2 employees. To qualify:
- You cannot count your day-job hours toward real estate hours
- Real estate hours must exceed all other work hours combined
- W-2 employee working 40 hrs/week (2,080 hrs/year) would need 2,081+ hours in real estate — effectively impossible while employed full-time

> **IRS scrutiny:** The IRS closely examines REP claims. Maintain contemporaneous time logs documenting every hour spent on real estate activities.

## Suspended (Carryforward) Losses

When passive losses exceed passive income in a given year, the excess loss is **suspended** (disallowed) and carried forward indefinitely.

**Suspended losses are deducted when:**

1. **Sufficient passive income is generated** in a future year (losses offset passive income)
2. **The property is sold** in a fully taxable disposition — all accumulated suspended losses become fully deductible in the year of sale, even against ordinary income

**Example:**
- Year 1: $20,000 rental loss, $0 passive income → $20,000 suspended
- Year 2: $15,000 rental loss → $35,000 total suspended
- Year 3: Sell property → $35,000 deductible in year of sale

> **Planning note:** The release of suspended losses at sale can significantly offset the taxable gain, including depreciation recapture.

## Form 8582 — Passive Activity Loss Limitations

**Form 8582** must be filed when:
- You have rental losses and your AGI may exceed $100,000
- You have suspended passive losses from prior years
- You are tracking multiple rental properties with different passive income/loss amounts

Form 8582 calculates:
- How much of the rental loss is allowed under the $25,000 exception
- How much is suspended and carried forward
- The aggregated passive income/loss across all passive activities

---

## Summary — Rental Loss Treatment by Scenario

| Scenario | Loss Treatment |
|---|---|
| AGI ≤ $100,000, actively participates | Up to $25,000 deductible against ordinary income |
| AGI $100,001–$149,999, actively participates | Partial deduction (phase-out applies) |
| AGI ≥ $150,000, actively participates | Suspended; carry forward |
| Qualifies as Real Estate Professional | All losses deductible against any income |
| Does not actively participate | All losses suspended; carry forward |
| Sells property (fully taxable) | All suspended losses released in year of sale |
