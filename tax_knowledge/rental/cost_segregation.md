# Cost Segregation Studies — Accelerated Depreciation for Real Property

**Primary IRS References:** Section 168(k) (Bonus Depreciation), Section 168 (MACRS), Rev. Proc. 87-56, Rev. Proc. 2002-9, Publication 946

---

## What Is a Cost Segregation Study?

A **cost segregation study** is an engineering-based tax analysis that reclassifies components of a real property building from the default 27.5-year (residential) or 39-year (commercial) depreciation schedule into **shorter-life asset categories** — 5, 7, or 15 years.

The result: **dramatically accelerated depreciation deductions** in the early years of ownership, often generating large front-loaded losses.

When combined with **bonus depreciation** (Section 168(k)), a cost seg study can produce a first-year deduction equal to 40–80% of the reclassified property value.

---

## How Asset Classes Work

Under MACRS, components of a building are categorized based on their nature and function:

| Asset Class | Examples | Depreciation Period |
|-------------|----------|-------------------|
| **Personal Property — 5-year** | Appliances, carpeting, certain fixtures, cabinets, window treatments, decorative items | **5 years** |
| **Personal Property — 7-year** | Office furniture and equipment inside rental | **7 years** |
| **Land Improvements — 15-year** | Landscaping, parking lots, fencing, sidewalks, outdoor lighting, swimming pool | **15 years** |
| **Building Structure — 27.5-year** | Foundation, framing, roof, exterior walls, plumbing, HVAC serving entire building | **27.5 years (residential)** |
| **Land** | Underlying land itself | **Not depreciable — ever** |

### What a Cost Seg Study Does

A qualified engineer physically inspects the property and separates:
- Which costs are 5-year personal property
- Which costs are 15-year land improvements
- What must remain as 27.5-year building structure

**Typical reclassification percentages:**
| Property Type | Reclassified to 5/7yr | Reclassified to 15yr | Remains 27.5yr |
|---------------|----------------------|---------------------|---------------|
| Single-family rental | 10–15% | 10–20% | 65–80% |
| Short-term rental (furnished) | **20–35%** | 10–15% | 50–70% |
| Commercial building | 15–25% | 10–15% | 60–75% |
| Newly constructed residential | 15–20% | 15–20% | 60–70% |

> STR properties typically have higher reclassification rates due to furnishings, appliances, and amenities included in the purchase.

## Interaction with Bonus Depreciation (Section 168(k))

**This is where cost seg becomes powerful.** All property reclassified to 5, 7, or 15 years qualifies for bonus depreciation — meaning a large percentage can be deducted **in the first year** rather than over 5-15 years.

### Bonus Depreciation Schedule (Post-TCJA)

| Tax Year | Bonus % | Notes |
|----------|---------|-------|
| 2022 | 100% | Full expensing |
| 2023 | 80% | Phase-down begins |
| 2024 | 60% | |
| **2025** | **40%** | Current year |
| 2026 | 20% | (unless extended by Congress) |
| 2027+ | 0% | Fully phased out (absent legislation) |

> **Act now:** Every year of delay reduces the bonus depreciation percentage available.

### Year-1 Impact Example

**Property:** $800,000 residential rental (building + land)  
**Land value:** $150,000 (not depreciable)  
**Depreciable basis:** $650,000

**Without cost seg (standard):**
- Year 1 depreciation: $650,000 ÷ 27.5 = **$23,636**

**With cost seg (2025):**
- 25% reclassified to 5/7yr: $162,500 × 40% bonus = $65,000 Year 1 + $97,500 over 5 yrs
- 15% reclassified to 15yr: $97,500 × 40% bonus = $39,000 Year 1 + $58,500 over 15 yrs
- Remaining 60% at 27.5yr: $390,000 ÷ 27.5 = $14,182 Year 1
- **Total Year 1: $65,000 + $39,000 + $14,182 = $118,182**

**Vs. $23,636 without cost seg = $94,546 additional first-year deduction**

At a 32% marginal rate: **~$30,000 in tax savings accelerated to Year 1**

## The Passive Activity Loss (PAL) Problem

> **⚠️ Critical:** Depreciation losses from rental property are **passive by default** under Section 469. If you can't use passive losses, cost seg just builds up a suspended loss carryforward — it doesn't help you today.

### Three Ways to Use Rental Losses

#### 1. AGI Under $150,000 — Active Participation Exception

If you **actively participate** in the rental and your MAGI is:
- **≤ $100,000:** Deduct up to $25,000 rental losses against ordinary income
- **$100,001–$149,999:** Partial deduction (phases out $0.50 per $1 over $100k)
- **≥ $150,000:** $0 — losses are suspended

Active participation means you make management decisions (approve tenants, set rents, approve repairs). Hiring a PM is fine. Low bar.

#### 2. Real Estate Professional Status (Section 469(c)(7))

All rental losses become **non-passive** if you qualify as a real estate professional:
- More than **750 hours/year** in real property trades/businesses
- Real estate hours > ALL other work hours combined

**Practical reality for LucraLab founders:** While running a full-time business, the "more than all other work combined" test is virtually impossible to meet.

#### 3. Short-Term Rental Exception — The STR Loophole ⭐

If the **average rental period is 7 days or less** AND you **materially participate** in the rental activity:
- The rental activity is treated as a **non-passive business activity**
- Losses offset W-2 income, business income, anything
- **No income cap. No hour threshold for real estate professional status.**

**Material participation for STR** — meet any ONE of the 7 tests (Reg. §1.469-5T):
1. 500+ hours/year in the activity
2. Substantially all activity performed by you
3. More than 100 hours AND more than any other individual
4. Significant participation activity (100+ hours) across multiple activities
5. Material participant in 5 of prior 10 years
6. Material participant in 3 of prior 10 years (personal services activity)
7. Facts and circumstances — regular, continuous, substantial involvement

> **Moses Lake (Wet Feet Retreat):** Average stay is well under 7 days. If James materially participates — responds to guests, manages bookings, coordinates cleaners, handles issues — this is a **non-passive activity.** Cost seg losses would be deductible against all income.

**Document this carefully:**
- Keep a time log of hours spent on STR management
- Correspondence with guests, vendors, cleaners counts
- Reviewing financials, managing listings, pricing strategy counts

## Lookback Studies — Catch-Up for Properties You Already Own

You don't need to do the study in Year 1. Under **Rev. Proc. 2002-9** (change in accounting method), you can conduct a cost seg study on a property you've owned for years and **catch up all previously missed depreciation in a single year** using a **Section 481(a) adjustment**.

**This is extremely powerful:**
- No need to amend prior returns
- The entire catch-up deduction hits in the year you file Form 3115 (Change in Accounting Method)
- Example: 10-year-old property — you get 10 years of accelerated depreciation in Year 1 of the study

**Form 3115** — Application for Change in Accounting Method
- File with your current-year return
- Automatic consent available for most cost segregation situations
- Include a signed copy with the return, send original to IRS Service Center

> This is how cost seg creates massive deductions on existing properties. No amended returns needed.

## Depreciation Recapture at Sale — The Hidden Cost

When you sell a property that's been depreciated, the IRS **recaptures** some of the deductions at sale.

### Section 1250 Recapture (Buildings)

- Standard 27.5-year residential depreciation: Recaptured at a maximum **25% rate** (unrecaptured Section 1250 gain)
- This is lower than ordinary income rates for most taxpayers

### Section 1245 Recapture (Personal Property)

- 5-year and 7-year personal property reclassified by cost seg: Recaptured as **ordinary income** (at your marginal rate) when sold
- This is the "cost" of accelerated depreciation — you pay back the tax savings at sale

### Net Impact Analysis

| Strategy | Year 1 Tax Benefit | Sale Recapture | Net Benefit |
|----------|-------------------|----------------|-------------|
| Standard 27.5-yr | Lower deduction | 25% on all depreciation | Baseline |
| Cost seg (keep property) | Large Year 1 deduction | Ordinary rate on 5-yr, 25% on 27.5-yr | Time value of money WIN |
| Cost seg + 1031 exchange | Large Year 1 deduction | **Deferred indefinitely** | Maximum benefit |

> **The 1031 strategy:** Do cost seg for Year 1 deductions → when selling, do a 1031 exchange into a new property → recapture is deferred indefinitely. See `1031_exchange.md`.

### When Recapture Doesn't Matter

- You hold the property until death → step-up in basis eliminates all depreciation recapture
- You do a 1031 exchange → recapture deferred to the next property
- You donate the property to charity → no recapture event

## Is a Cost Seg Study Right for Your Properties?

### Financial Viability Thresholds

| Property Value | Study Cost Range | Typically Worth It? |
|----------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| < $300,000 | $3,000–$5,000 | Rarely — ROI too low |
| $300k–$600k | $3,500–$6,000 | Maybe — depends on PAL situation |
| $600k–$1M | $5,000–$8,000 | Usually yes |
| > $1M | $6,000–$12,000 | Yes, almost always |

**Accelerator factors that increase ROI:**
- Newer property (more personal property, higher reclassification %)
- STR property (more furnishings/appliances)
- High marginal tax rate (32%+)
- Ability to USE the losses (STR exception, AGI < $150k, or passive income to offset)
- Planning to hold long-term or use 1031 exchange at sale

### Your Properties — Quick Assessment

| Property | Est. Value | PAL Situation | Cost Seg Viability |
|----------|-----------|---------------|-------------------|
| Moses Lake (WFR) | ~$600k? | **STR exception — losses non-passive** | ✅ **Strong candidate** |
| Port Orchard | ~$350k? | Passive (LTR, need AGI check) | Marginal |
| Shelby NC | ~$150k? | Passive | Likely too small |
| Auburn Duplex | ~$400k? | Passive (need AGI check) | Maybe — if caught-up |

> For Port Orchard and Auburn: A lookback study could produce a large Year 1 catch-up deduction. Value depends on your AGI and whether you have passive income to absorb the losses.

## How to Get a Study Done

### What to Look for in a Provider

- Must be performed by a **qualified engineer or architect** (the IRS requires this)
- Report should include component-by-component itemization
- Should cover field inspection + engineering analysis
- Provide Form 3115 support (if catch-up study on existing property)

### Types of Providers

| Provider Type | Cost Range | Notes |
