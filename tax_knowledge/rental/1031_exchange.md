# Like-Kind Exchange — Section 1031

**Primary IRS Reference:** Section 1031, Publication 544, Form 8824

---

## Overview

A **Section 1031 like-kind exchange** allows you to defer capital gains tax (and depreciation recapture) when you sell investment property, as long as you reinvest the proceeds into a qualifying replacement property.

**What gets deferred:**
- Long-term capital gains on the sale
- Unrecaptured Section 1250 gain (25% depreciation recapture)
- Net Investment Income Tax (3.8%) on the deferred gain

**What does NOT qualify:**
- Personal residence (primary home)
- Inventory (property held for sale, e.g., by a developer/flipper)
- Partnership interests (generally)
- Stocks, bonds, or other securities

---

## Core Rules — All Four Must Be Met

### 1. Like-Kind Property

Replacement property must be "like-kind" to the relinquished property.

**For real estate:** The standard is **very broad** — any real property located in the United States qualifies as like-kind to any other U.S. real property.

**Valid exchanges:**
- Single-family rental → apartment complex
- Vacant land → office building
- Rental house → shopping center
- Farmland → rental condo

**No longer valid post-TCJA (2018):**
- Personal property (equipment, vehicles, artwork) — Section 1031 now applies only to real property

### 2. 45-Day Identification Deadline

You must **identify** potential replacement properties in writing within **45 calendar days** of the closing date of the relinquished property.

**Identification rules:**
- Written, signed notice to the Qualified Intermediary (QI) or seller
- **3-property rule:** Identify up to 3 properties of any value
- **200% rule:** Identify any number of properties as long as total FMV does not exceed 200% of the relinquished property's value
- **95% rule:** Identify any number of properties if you actually receive 95%+ of total FMV identified

> **No extensions.** The 45-day deadline is strict — it cannot be extended even if it falls on a weekend or holiday (though some narrow disaster relief exceptions exist).

### 3. 180-Day Closing Deadline

You must **close on** (acquire) the replacement property within **180 calendar days** of selling the relinquished property (or by your tax return due date including extensions, whichever is earlier).

> **Important:** If your return is due before 180 days, file an extension (Form 4868) to preserve the full 180 days.

### 4. Qualified Intermediary (QI) Required

The proceeds from the sale **cannot pass through your hands**. A **Qualified Intermediary** must:

- Hold the sale proceeds in escrow
- Transfer funds at closing to acquire the replacement property
- Prepare and maintain exchange documentation

If you receive the cash — even briefly — the exchange is **disqualified** and the full gain becomes taxable immediately.

> **No QI = no 1031.** This is a non-negotiable requirement. Select a reputable QI before listing the property for sale.

---

## Boot — Taxable Proceeds

**Boot** is any non-like-kind property received in the exchange, including cash. Boot is taxable to the extent of your realized gain.

**Types of boot:**
- Cash received from the exchange (mortgage relief, price difference)
- Net mortgage relief: if new property has less debt than old property, the difference is boot
- Other personal property received

**Example:**
- Sold rental for $500,000, mortgage paid off $100,000, net to QI = $400,000
- Bought replacement for $450,000 (with new $50,000 mortgage + $400,000 QI funds)
- Boot = $0 (reinvested all proceeds, new debt ≥ old debt)

**Example with boot:**
- Sold for $500,000, original basis $200,000, realized gain $300,000
- Bought replacement for $450,000 ($50,000 cash received as boot)
- Taxable gain = $50,000 (the boot received); $250,000 gain deferred

---

## Depreciation Basis in a 1031 Exchange

The tax basis in the replacement property **does not reset** to fair market value. Instead:

**Replacement property basis = FMV − Deferred Gain**

Or equivalently:
**Replacement property basis = Relinquished property's adjusted basis + Boot paid + Gain recognized − Boot received**

**Consequence:** The lower carryover basis means **higher future depreciation recapture** when you eventually sell.

**Example:**
- Relinquished property: FMV $500,000, adjusted basis $200,000
- Replacement property: FMV $500,000
- Replacement property tax basis: $200,000 (not $500,000)
- Annual depreciation: $200,000 ÷ 27.5 = $7,273/year (not $500,000 ÷ 27.5 = $18,182)

---

## Ultimate Sale — Deferred Gain Becomes Taxable

When you sell the replacement property in a taxable sale (no 1031):
- All deferred gain from prior exchanges becomes taxable
- Plus any additional gain on the replacement property
- Plus all accumulated depreciation recapture

## Estate Planning — 1031 + Step-Up at Death

**Most powerful planning strategy:**

1. Exchange properties repeatedly throughout life (deferring all gains)
2. Hold the final property until death
3. Heirs receive a **stepped-up basis** to fair market value at date of death (Section 1014)
4. All deferred gains and depreciation recapture are **permanently eliminated**

This combination makes rental real estate one of the most tax-efficient investment vehicles available.

> **Note:** Step-up at death is subject to potential legislative change. Monitor for proposals to limit or eliminate the step-up basis provision.

## Reverse and Improvement Exchanges

### Reverse Exchange (Buy First, Sell Second)
- Uses an **Exchange Accommodation Titleholder (EAT)** to hold replacement property
- Must complete sale of relinquished property within **180 days** of acquiring replacement
- More complex and expensive; requires experienced QI

### Improvement Exchange (Build-to-Suit)
- Allows using exchange proceeds to **improve** the replacement property
- Improvements must be completed within the 180-day exchange period
- QI holds title and funds improvements; transfers completed property to taxpayer
