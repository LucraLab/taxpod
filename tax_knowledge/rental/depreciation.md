# Rental Property Depreciation

**Primary IRS References:** Publication 946, Section 168 (MACRS), Section 168(k) (Bonus Depreciation), Section 179, Section 1250

Depreciation is typically the **largest single deduction** available to rental property owners. It allows you to recover the cost of the property over its IRS-prescribed useful life, even if the property is actually appreciating in market value.

---

## MACRS Depreciation — The Standard Method

The **Modified Accelerated Cost Recovery System (MACRS)** governs depreciation for property placed in service after 1986.

### Recovery Periods

| Property Type | Recovery Period | Method |
|---|---|---|
| Residential rental property (structure) | **27.5 years** | Straight-line |
| Commercial/nonresidential property (structure) | **39 years** | Straight-line |
| Appliances, carpeting, furniture (personal property in rental) | **5 years** | 200% DB |
| Land improvements (fences, landscaping, sidewalks) | **15 years** | 150% DB |
| Land itself | **Never depreciable** | N/A |

### Residential vs. Commercial

- **Residential rental:** ≥ 80% of gross rental income comes from dwelling units
- **Commercial rental:** Less than 80% from dwelling units, or mixed commercial/residential

---

## Land Allocation — Critical Rule

**Land is NEVER depreciable.** You must allocate the purchase price between land and building.

**How to allocate:**
1. Use the property tax assessment ratio (land value ÷ total assessed value)
2. Use an independent appraisal
3. Use the sales contract if it separately states land and building values

**Example:**
- Purchase price: $300,000
- Property tax assessment: land $50,000, building $250,000 → ratio 83.3% building
- Depreciable basis: $300,000 × 83.3% = **$250,000**
- Annual depreciation: $250,000 ÷ 27.5 = **$9,091/year**

> **Common mistake:** Depreciating the full purchase price without allocating land. The IRS will catch this on audit and may assess penalties.

## Placed-in-Service Date

Depreciation begins when the property is **"placed in service"** — meaning it is ready and available for rent, even if no tenant has moved in yet.

- Property is vacant but listed for rent → placed in service
- Property needs repairs before renting → NOT placed in service until repairs are complete
- You must actually offer the property for rent (advertising, available for lease)

### Mid-Month Convention

Residential rental and commercial property use the **mid-month convention**: the property is treated as placed in service (or disposed of) in the **middle of the month**, regardless of the actual date.

**Example:** Property placed in service October 10 → treated as October 15 for depreciation purposes → only 2.5 months of depreciation in year 1 (Oct 15 – Dec 31).

**First-year depreciation table (27.5-year residential):**

## Component Depreciation (Cost Segregation)

Certain components of a rental property may qualify for **shorter depreciation periods**:

- **Appliances, carpeting, window treatments:** 5 years (personal property)
- **Land improvements:** 15 years
- **Building structure:** 27.5 years (residential)

A **cost segregation study** identifies these components, accelerating depreciation deductions. Typically worthwhile for properties with depreciable basis > $500,000.

## Bonus Depreciation — Section 168(k)

Bonus depreciation allows an **additional first-year deduction** on eligible property.

### Phase-Down Schedule (Post-TCJA)

| Year | Bonus Depreciation Rate |
|---|---|
| 2018–2022 | 100% |
| 2023 | 80% |
| 2024 | 60% |
| **2025** | **40%** |
| 2026 | 20% |
| 2027+ | 0% (unless extended by Congress) |

### Key Rules for Bonus Depreciation

- **Applies to:** Personal property (5-year and 15-year MACRS property) — appliances, carpeting, land improvements, cost-segregated components
- **Does NOT apply to:** The residential rental structure itself (27.5-year property)
- **Can create a loss** (unlike Section 179)
- Applies to new **and used** property (as long as taxpayer hasn't previously used it)
- No income limitation

**Example (2025):**
- Purchase $10,000 of appliances for a new rental unit
- Bonus depreciation: $10,000 × 40% = $4,000 deducted in year one
- Remaining $6,000 depreciated over 5 years (MACRS)

## Section 179 Expensing

Section 179 allows **immediate expensing** of qualifying property in the year placed in service.

### 2023/2024 Limits

| Year | Deduction Limit | Phase-Out Begins |
|---|---|---|
| 2023 | $1,160,000 | $2,890,000 of assets placed in service |
| 2024 | $1,220,000 | $3,050,000 of assets placed in service |

### Section 179 for Rental Property

- **Residential rental property structure (27.5-year):** NOT eligible for Section 179
- **Personal property used in a rental (appliances, etc.):** Generally eligible, but **limited to taxable income from business** — Section 179 cannot create a loss
- **Qualified improvement property (QIP):** Eligible for Section 179 for non-residential rentals

> **Tip:** For rental property personal assets, bonus depreciation (Section 168(k)) is usually more advantageous than Section 179 because it CAN create a loss.

## Depreciation Recapture — Section 1250

When you **sell** a rental property, the IRS "recaptures" the depreciation deductions you've taken.

- **Section 1250 recapture rate: 25%** (unrecaptured Section 1250 gain)
- Applies to all depreciation claimed on real property (the structure, not land)
- This is the portion of your capital gain taxed at 25%, not the standard long-term capital gain rate

**Example:**
- Purchased for $300,000 ($250,000 depreciable basis)
- Held 10 years: accumulated depreciation = $90,909
- Adjusted basis: $300,000 − $90,909 = $209,091
- Sold for $400,000 → total gain = $190,909
- Unrecaptured Section 1250 gain: $90,909 taxed at 25% = $22,727 tax
- Remaining gain: $100,000 taxed at capital gain rates (0%, 15%, or 20%)

> **Important:** You must recapture depreciation even if you never claimed it. The IRS calculates recapture based on depreciation "allowed or allowable."
