# Deductible Rental Expenses

**Primary IRS Reference:** Publication 527, Schedule E (Form 1040)

All ordinary and necessary expenses for managing, conserving, and maintaining rental property are deductible on **Schedule E, Part I**.

---

## Deductible Expense Categories

### Advertising
- Cost of ads to find tenants (online listings, newspaper, signs)
- Fully deductible in year paid

### Auto and Travel
- Mileage or actual expenses for travel **to and from the rental property** for management, maintenance, or collection
- **Standard mileage rate:** $0.67/mile (2024) — requires mileage log
- **Actual expense method:** gas, insurance, depreciation, maintenance (business-use percentage)
- Local transportation between properties is deductible
- Overnight travel: meals (50%) and lodging deductible if primary purpose is rental management

### Cleaning and Maintenance
- Routine cleaning between tenants
- Lawn care, snow removal, pest control
- Regular upkeep costs (not improvements — see Repairs vs. Improvements below)

### Commissions
- Leasing commissions paid to brokers or agents to find tenants
- Fully deductible in year paid

### Depreciation
- **The single largest rental deduction** — see `depreciation.md` for full details
- Residential rental property: 27.5-year straight-line (MACRS)
- Claimed on Form 4562; flows to Schedule E

### HOA Fees
- Homeowners Association fees paid for the rental property
- Fully deductible in year paid

### Insurance
- Fire insurance
- Flood insurance
- Liability insurance (landlord policy)
- Theft and casualty insurance
- **Prepaid premiums:** deduct only the portion allocable to the current tax year

### Legal and Professional Fees
- Attorney fees for lease preparation, tenant disputes, evictions
- Accountant fees for rental-related tax preparation
- Property management consultation fees
- **Note:** Legal fees for acquiring the property are NOT deductible — they are added to the property's basis

### Management Fees
- Fees paid to a property management company
- Typically 8–12% of monthly gross rents
- Fully deductible

### Mortgage Interest
- Interest on loans used to acquire or improve the rental property
- Reported by lender on **Form 1098**
- **Only the interest portion** is deductible — principal payments are NOT deductible
- Home equity loan interest is deductible only if proceeds were used for the rental property

### Points
- Points paid to obtain a mortgage on a rental property are **NOT** fully deductible in the year paid
- Must be **amortized (spread) over the life of the loan**
- Example: $3,000 in points on a 30-year mortgage = $100/year deduction

### Property Taxes
- Real estate taxes assessed by state/local governments
- Deductible in year paid (cash basis)
- Special assessments for local benefits (sidewalks, sewers) are NOT deductible — added to basis

### Repairs
- Costs to keep property in good working condition (not to add value or extend useful life)
- Deductible in full in the year paid
- See **Repairs vs. Improvements** section below

### Supplies
- Cleaning supplies, small tools, materials for minor repairs
- Fully deductible in year purchased

### Utilities
- If the **landlord pays** utilities (water, trash, electricity, gas): fully deductible
- If the **tenant pays** utilities directly: not deductible (not your expense)
- Common in multi-family properties or furnished rentals

---

## Repairs vs. Improvements — Critical Distinction

### Repairs (Deduct in Year Paid)
Repairs restore property to its original working condition without adding value or extending useful life.

**Examples of repairs:**
- Fix a broken window or door
- Patch a roof leak (not full replacement)
- Repaint interior or exterior
- Fix a leaky faucet or running toilet
- Replace a broken appliance part
- Repair damaged flooring (patch, not full replacement)

### Improvements (Must Depreciate)
Improvements add value, extend useful life, or adapt property to a new use. They are capitalized and depreciated over their useful life.

**Examples of improvements:**
- New roof (full replacement)
- Room addition
- HVAC system replacement
- New flooring (full replacement throughout)
- Kitchen or bathroom remodel
- New appliances (though Section 179 / bonus depreciation may allow immediate expensing — see `depreciation.md`)
- Fence, deck, or patio addition

### Small Taxpayer Safe Harbor — $2,500 Rule
Under IRS regulations (Reg. §1.263(a)-1(f)), taxpayers **without an applicable financial statement** can elect to expense items that cost **$2,500 or less per item or invoice**.

- Must make the election each year on the tax return
- Applies per item or per invoice (whichever is less)
- Allows landlords to immediately deduct many items that would otherwise be improvements
- **Example:** Replace a water heater for $1,800 → can expense immediately rather than depreciate

> **Tip:** Keep separate invoices for items under $2,500 to take advantage of this safe harbor.

---

## Expenses NOT Deductible on Schedule E

- **Property purchase price** (add to basis; recover through depreciation)
- **Mortgage principal payments**
- **Personal use portion** of expenses for mixed-use properties
- **Travel to inspect property before purchase**
- **Legal fees for acquiring property** (added to basis)
- **Improvements** (capitalized and depreciated, not expensed — unless safe harbor applies)
